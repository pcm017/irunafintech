# AU Small Finance Bank

## One-liner

India's largest small finance bank by asset size, in the middle of a live transition to a universal banking license, with a retail-heavy secured/unsecured mixed lending book (wheels, MBL, home loans, gold loans, MFI, credit cards) built up organically and via the Fincare SFB merger.

## Current state

Q4FY26 (April 27, 2026): AU SFB delivered its best-ever quarterly print — PAT ₹832 cr (+65% YoY, +25% QoQ), RoA 1.8%, RoE 17.0% — hitting the RoA level management had guided for FY27 a full year early. NIM inflected upward, expanding 16–24 bps QoQ to 5.96% after a FY26 compression to 5.65% (from 5.94% FY25), on lower cost of funds (down 12 bps QoQ to 6.49%) and seasonal recoveries. Credit cost normalized sharply to 0.96% of average assets for FY26 (from 1.30% FY25) as MFI and unsecured/credit-card stress worked through the system — but management explicitly guided the Street to model FY27 credit cost around ~90 bps rather than extrapolate the stronger Q4 print, a deliberately conservative signal.

The single largest structural development is the **universal bank transition**: RBI amended the NOFHC (holding company) requirement so it now applies only if AU or its promoter group sets up a new group entity in future — removing a key regulatory friction point. AU filed its final universal banking license application in March 2026 and awaits approval, with no committed timeline. If granted, this would make AU the first small finance bank in India's system to transition to a universal bank, unlocking a broader product mandate (credit cards, corporate banking) and — per CEO Sanjay Agarwal's stated long-term ambition — a path to cost of funds converging toward the prevailing repo rate (5.25% vs AU's current 6.75%, a ~150 bps structural gap).

Deposits grew 23% YoY to ₹1.52 lakh cr against an estimated ~13% private-banking-sector growth rate, while advances grew 21% YoY to ₹1.40 lakh cr, led by the secured book (retail + commercial, +23% YoY); unsecured (MFI + personal loans) returned to sequential growth (+7% QoQ) after a prior slowdown, though still down 1% YoY.

## Thesis / view

AU SFB is at an inflection point on two fronts simultaneously: the FY26 NIM-compression/credit-cost-normalization cycle (shared with peers like *kotak mahindra bank*) appears to have bottomed in Q3FY26, and a structural regulatory catalyst (universal bank transition) is now filed and awaiting approval rather than speculative. The combination — cyclical recovery plus a filed structural catalyst — is a more concrete setup than most SFB-to-bank transition stories, which are typically framed as multi-year aspirations without a live application.

The bull case rests on cost-of-funds convergence: AU's 6.75% FY26 cost of funds sits ~150 bps above the ~5.25% repo rate and ~100 bps above mid-sized bank levels (~6.25%), and management has explicitly named repo-rate convergence as the long-term target once "mature" as a universal bank. That gap, if compressed even partially, is a direct NIM tailwind on top of the already-recovering 5.65% FY26 NIM. The bear case is timing risk — management repeatedly declined to commit to an approval timeline, and CEO commentary describes repo-rate cost-of-funds parity as a "long-term dream," not a near-term deliverable.

Management's credibility signal in this print is proactive guidance conservatism: rather than letting the Street extrapolate a strong Q4 (RoA already at the FY27-guided level, credit cost improving faster than plan), Sanjay Agarwal anchored FY27 credit cost expectations around 90 bps — well above the Q4 print — and declined to firm up universal-bank timing. This is worth tracking against realized numbers over the next several quarters as a test of management's stated "automatic" risk-pullback underwriting model, particularly if the below-normal FY27 monsoon flagged across other lending names in this wiki (see *bank of baroda*, *kotak mahindra bank*) materializes and pressures the rural/agri-linked segments of AU's secured book.

## Contradictions

Flagged (2026-07-07): the investor presentation's credit-cost bridge shows Q4FY26 credit cost of ₹269 cr = 0.15% of average total assets, while management's verbal commentary on the earnings call describes the same quarter's credit cost as 0.60%. This likely reflects different denominators or annualization conventions (quarterly vs. annualized-quarterly), but the two disclosures were not reconciled within the available source material. Needs verification against the next quarter's presentation, which should show comparable prior-period figures.
