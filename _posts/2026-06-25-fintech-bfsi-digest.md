## Key Data / Quotes

### The Deregulation Paradox (Fintech Takes / Alex Johnson) — Full Extract

**The CFPB situation:** The CFPB has been effectively defanged — supervision paused, enforcement slowed, budget cut. But compliance teams at banks haven't been reduced. Why? Because the **look-back period for financial regulation is five years.** Statutes haven't changed. A bank that violates consumer protection laws today can still be sued in 2030. Institutions are acting like the current regulatory posture is temporary — because it probably is.

**The new bank charter question:** Faster charter approvals, flexible business models, potentially lower capital requirements. But you can't deregulate BSA/AML — those requirements come from Treasury, not banking regulators, and they're not changing. A new bank charter still faces the same AML obligations as a 150-year-old bank.

**On stablecoins and AML:** Stablecoin issuers under the current legislation would be regulated as money transmitters — BSA/AML obligations apply. But unlike traditional money transmitters, they're potentially onboarding millions via permissionless protocols. **"TD Bank's settlement last year — $3 billion, with criminal charges — was a case study in what happens when a financial institution fails to build adequate AML controls while growing rapidly. The concern is that stablecoin issuers may be taking on TD Bank-level AML exposure without the operational infrastructure to manage it."**

**What deregulation actually looks like:** Real deregulation — the kind that changes behaviour — happens at the examination level. Early signs of shift at OCC and FDIC (from remediation of every finding to focusing on material issues). Whether it continues depends on who runs the agencies in two years.

**Bottom line:** "The financial system is not actually being deregulated in the meaningful sense. Risk is being transferred, not reduced. When the CFPB doesn't enforce consumer protection laws, that doesn't make the conduct legal. It means the enforcement mechanism has changed — from regulatory action to private litigation and state AGs. Which may actually be more unpredictable and expensive for financial institutions in the long run."

Western Union sees opportunity in stablecoins-as-payments-infrastructure (not speculative assets) — leverages their existing AML/compliance infrastructure and correspondent banking relationships that new stablecoin issuers would need to build from scratch.

### UPI Feature Update
**Beneficiary name display**: RBI directive now live — UPI shows the bank-registered name when scanning a QR code or entering a mobile number, replacing the merchant's custom alias. Aimed at reducing fraud from fake merchant QR codes and alias impersonation. Small but meaningful: friction added at the point of payment initiation, with fraud-reduction payoff.

## Signals

- **The Deregulation Paradox has a direct India analogy in RBI's approach:** India's RBI has consistently maintained that it does not deregulate systemic risk — it only simplifies compliance architecture while keeping underlying obligations intact. The Fintech Takes framing of "risk transfer not risk reduction" when regulators step back maps cleanly onto RBI's philosophy: the risk of consumer harm from NBFC defaults, co-lending failures, or digital lending fraud doesn't disappear when RBI tightens or loosens — it migrates. India's 135 NBFC licence cancellations in June 2026 are the Indian equivalent of moving enforcement to the examination level — systemic risk addressed through registration cleanup rather than rule changes
- **Stablecoin AML = the missing piece of India's stablecoin policy debate:** India has oscillated between crypto bans and regulatory neutrality; the BIS Chapter III + Alex Johnson's TD Bank framing makes the specific risk concrete. Any Indian bank or payments company exploring stablecoin rails will need to address: (1) FinCEN-equivalent AML obligations, (2) real-time transaction monitoring at permissionless-protocol scale, and (3) sanctions screening for stablecoin-facilitated cross-border flows. RBI's existing AML framework (PMLA + FATF guidance) applies but stablecoin-specific implementation guidelines don't exist yet
- **INR at 92.5-97 range reflects genuine uncertainty:** A 450bps weekly range forecast (Kotak) is unusually wide and reflects three competing forces: (a) DXY 101.6 pushing INR weaker, (b) Brent/WTI falling (crude-import CAD improvement pushing INR stronger), and (c) FII equity outflows (-₹43K Cr MTD) creating structural selling. The outcome depends on which force dominates: if Fed rate hike repricing continues, DXY and US yield headwinds dominate and INR tests 97; if Brent falls to $65-70 and CAD improves, INR can reach 92.5 by Q3 2026
- **PBoC Lujiazui framing:** Pan Gongsheng's "financial structure evolution" speech likely addresses China's push for (1) RMB internationalisation, (2) CIPS (China's alternative to SWIFT) expansion, and (3) digital yuan cross-border pilot expansion. Each of these is a competitive vector against India's UPI-for-the-world narrative
