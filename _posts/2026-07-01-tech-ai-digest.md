## Key Data / Quotes

**India AI sovereignty ("Intelligence Independence" — The Ken, Rohin Dharmakumar):**
- Anthropic's Mythos was rolled back after the Trump administration treated its capability claims literally; access cut for non-US paying customers; repackaged as Fable for the "masses," which was also disabled
- Fable now available to 100+ American companies and government agencies "approved" by the administration
- OpenAI's GPT 5.6 following the same pattern — available only to a select set of US-approved companies; **no access yet for India or Indians**
- India is nominally part of "Pax Silica" (signed Feb 2026) — a US-led "strategic counter-narrative to restrictive, fear-based regulatory regimes" — but this has not translated into confirmed access
- **Bernstein** warned of a potential India **"AI blackout"** — risk that "the most powerful applications in finance or military are no longer working" if foreign-lab API/weight access vanishes
- **GLM-5.2** (China's Z.ai/Zhipu): near-Anthropic-frontier quality at ~1/8th the cost, open-weight; called "the first open model that passes the bar as a daily driver" (ex-Google DeepMind VP); also praised by Vercel CEO Guillermo Rauch
- **Fugu** (Japan's Sakana): a novel "model-of-models" approach
- Indian cloud providers (Yotta, E2E Networks) mostly rent bare-metal NVIDIA H100/B200 GPUs rather than offering per-token billing — structural gap for serving open-weight alternatives at startup scale
- Sarvam (India's "sovereign foundational model") has shown limited public benchmark confidence
- The Ken running a live "Intelligence Independence" event Aug 1, Bangalore

## Signals

**1. The Ken's "Intelligence Independence" piece is the most detailed articulation yet of India's structural AI-access risk.** Prior wiki entries flagged the Mythos/Fable pullback and GPT-5.6 gating as governance events; this piece adds the concrete India-specific consequence — despite India's nominal inclusion in the Feb 2026 "Pax Silica" declaration, there is no confirmed India access to either Anthropic's or OpenAI's latest restricted models. Bernstein's "AI blackout" framing (finance/military applications going dark if foreign-lab access vanishes) elevates this from a competitiveness concern to a national-security-adjacent risk. This directly reinforces the India diplomatic assurance-seeking already tracked (June 27) and gives it sharper stakes.

**2. GLM-5.2's emergence as a credible near-frontier, 1/8th-cost, open-weight alternative changes India's hedging calculus.** If GLM-5.2 quality claims hold up under independent evaluation, it becomes a genuine fallback option for India enterprises and government agencies facing US-model access uncertainty — but the infrastructure gap (Indian clouds lacking per-token billing, mostly renting bare-metal GPUs) means India cannot yet operationalise this hedge at startup/SME scale. This is the concrete "chicken-and-egg" problem underlying India's sovereign-AI ambitions: even when an alternative model exists, the serving infrastructure to democratise access domestically is not yet built.

**3. TSMC's capex guidance ($52-56B, up 27-40% YoY) is materially larger than the "$45B approved" figure flagged in the June 30 digest** — likely reflecting the same underlying capacity-expansion decision reported with different scope/rounding across sources. Either way, the direction is consistent: TSMC is expanding aggressively while capacity remains fully committed through 2028, and Samsung Foundry is absorbing the overflow demand (Google, NVIDIA, Tesla, AMD, BYD). Tesla's A16 going exclusively to Samsung Texas is a notable single-customer commitment that validates Samsung's advanced-node comeback narrative.

**4. Intel Foundry gaining explicit White House backing introduces a third credible foundry option, complicating Samsung's recovery narrative.** With Apple already exploring diversification to both Samsung and Intel Foundry, the advanced-node foundry market may be moving from a TSMC-dominant structure toward a genuine three-way competitive dynamic (TSMC, Samsung, Intel) for the first time in years — a longer-term positive for AI chip supply resilience but a near-term source of execution and allocation uncertainty for customers.

**5. India's semiconductor mission has scaled to 12 approved facilities (~₹1.64 lakh crore) with concrete production milestones now materialising, not just announced.** Micron's Sanand ATMP (operational Feb 28) and Kaynes Semicon's OSAT (commercial production March 2026, just 14 months from groundbreaking) demonstrate India can execute back-end semiconductor manufacturing on a credible timeline. The flagship Tata Electronics-PSMC Dholera fab's December 2026 first-silicon target is the next major credibility test — prior wiki entries have flagged repeated India fab timeline slippage risk, making this the single most important semiconductor milestone to watch through year-end.
