## Signals

**1. WTI below $70 — Iran peace dividend maxing out, but capex caution emerging (Sizing: India macro dominant)**
WTI at $69.40 (-3.51%) is the lowest since before the US-Iran conflict; Persian Gulf exports are now at ~75% of pre-war levels. At this crude price, India's annualised import savings vs. the May $111 WTI peak exceed $170B — the macro relief is at maximum magnitude. The concurrent India G-Sec 10Y at 6.70% (13-week low) confirms bond markets are pricing in a lower inflation trajectory. However, the Corporate India CFO framing emerging simultaneously is defensive: "protect cash, maintain financial flexibility, prioritise resilience over efficiency." This signals the macro relief has not yet unlocked a capex investment cycle — corporate India is waiting for the WPI-CPI gap to close before committing growth investment.

**2. Nasdaq weekly -4.6% / KOSPI -5.81% — AI government restriction = new regime signal (Change: material; Root Cause: Trump AI access framework; Impact: India AI ecosystem sovereign access risk)**
The weekly Nasdaq -4.6% decline is directly attributable to the Trump administration restricting OpenAI's GPT-5.6 (Sol/Terra/Luna) rollout — demanding customer-by-customer government approval before access. This represents a de facto prior-approval regime for frontier AI model releases. KOSPI's -5.81% underperformance reflects semiconductor supply chain concerns (Samsung, SK Hynix exposure). India's response: the government is seeking diplomatic assurances from the US that AI chips and model access will not be curtailed. Bank Nifty's +1.69% divergence from Nifty's -0.21% signals Indian equity market is rotating toward domestic financial sector names, not tech.

**3. WPI-CPI gap: 5.77pp — India margin compression is the leading corporate risk for H1 FY27 (Change: gap widening; Root Cause: gold/silver distorting headline CPI + Brent-driven WPI; Impact: corporate margin squeeze without consumer pricing power)**
Core CPI at 2.3% (ex-gold/silver) is the critical signal: gold and silver are responsible for almost all of the headline CPI at 3.93%. This gives RBI cover to hold (or cut) even as WPI runs at 9.7%. But the WPI-CPI gap means Indian corporates are experiencing input cost pressure without passing it through — margins compressing. The RBI Pulse Survey makes this quantitative: 80%+ firms with higher costs, 70% with thinner margins. Resolution path: either Brent falls further (easing WPI) or eventually price hikes flow through to CPI. The falling crude trajectory supports the first path being the dominant one.

**4. India G-Sec 10Y at 6.70% (13-week low) — FII debt momentum + rate cut expectations compressing yields (Change: -14bps from prior 6.84%; Root Cause: crude deflation → lower inflation expectations → G-Sec demand; Impact: August RBI rate cut probability rising)**
The India 10Y at 6.70% is the single most important India financial data point this week. It reflects three forces compressing simultaneously: (a) crude oil deflation reducing CPI risk; (b) strong FII debt inflows on RBI's three-pillar capital playbook (FCNR(B) + G-Sec exemption + LCR pricing); (c) growing market consensus for an August RBI rate cut given CPI at 3.93% and Brent below $70. At 6.70%, the India-US spread (+230bps) remains attractive for foreign bond buyers — EM carry demand intact.

**5. FinTech 2.0 — sector consolidation signals a new credit-quality-over-growth regime (Change: India fintech funding $8B → $2B over 5 years; 700+ fintechs shut; Root Cause: post-growth-at-any-cost correction; Impact: NPCI credit-on-UPI next rails, but NBFC exclusion is policy bottleneck)**
The FinTech 2.0 narrative consolidates the structural shift in India's fintech sector. NPCI building credit-on-UPI rails is the next payments infrastructure play, but the bank-only restriction limits the ramp. NSE filing its DRHP (June 17; ₹30,000 crore est.) is the capital markets signal — exchange monetisation of the retail derivatives boom, but the F&O revenue concentration risk (60% of operating revenue) is also the existential regulatory risk.
