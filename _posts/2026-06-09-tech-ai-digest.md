## Signals

**Apollo $35B = AI compute is now a credit product.** Debt markets are financing AI infrastructure at scale — the AI capex supercycle has crossed from equity financing (VC rounds, hyperscaler capex) into leveraged debt. Broadcom backstopping senior tranches is structurally similar to semiconductor equipment leasing — the chip maker effectively finances the customer's hardware. Implications: (1) AI compute demand is durable enough for 7–10 year debt structures; (2) Anthropic's balance sheet is being scaled to compete with hyperscaler compute without equity dilution; (3) debt capital markets will increasingly be an AI infrastructure tool — not just VC/equity.

**Broadcom guidance miss — AI chip demand reality check.** The $1.2B gap ($16B vs $17.2B guidance) and scope narrowing (chips only, not integrated systems) signals that the custom AI ASIC ramp for hyperscalers is progressing but slower than the most optimistic sell-side models. This doesn't invalidate the AI infrastructure thesis but resets near-term multiples. Nvidia's position is less affected (GPU is the standard; custom ASICs are the alternative), but the entire sector sold off on contagion. Key question: is the $16B guidance a temporary pause or a trajectory revision?

**Apple WWDC — distribution over capability.** Apple's 2.5 billion device installed base is the world's largest closed-loop distribution network for an AI assistant. The strategy shift — integrating OpenAI/Anthropic rather than building natively — suggests Apple has decided the make-vs-buy question on AI models in favour of buy/partner. This is a significant customer acquisition victory for Anthropic and OpenAI (tens of millions of new users through Siri integration). For India specifically: iPhone's premium demographic in India (upper middle class, digital-first) will now get Anthropic/OpenAI AI capabilities baked in via Siri — accelerating AI assistant adoption in the premium segment.

**Unitree threat to Western robotics.** The $16–20K vs $100K+ price gap is not a marginal difference — it's a 5–6x cost advantage that enables a completely different addressable market. If Unitree can match on capability for 70–80% of use cases, the industrial/manufacturing robotics market globally shifts toward China. India implication: India's nascent robotics/automation manufacturing push (ATAL, PLI for electronics) faces a Unitree-shaped competitive threat in its domestic market. India's defence-linked robotics program (Bharat Forge, Tata Advanced Systems) would compete in different segments.

**EU Chips Act failure → India's ATMP-first approach vindicated.** Europe tried to build leading-edge fabs (Intel Germany, TSMC Dresden) and failed to attract ecosystem. India's stated approach — ATMP (Advanced Test, Mark, and Package) first, fab later — is more defensible because it matches India's actual demand and skill base. The EU lesson: massive subsidies without domestic demand are insufficient. India's fab ambitions (Tata Electronics Dholera) should be evaluated against the EU failure case.

**India AI traffic lag — opportunity or gap?** India still human-dominated web traffic while AI agents drive more than human traffic globally. This is partly structural (mobile video consumption pattern) but also reflects lower AI agent adoption in Indian enterprise and developer communities. As India's B2B SaaS, fintech, and IT services sectors adopt agentic workflows, India's web traffic composition will shift — but with a 12–24 month lag vs. global peers.
