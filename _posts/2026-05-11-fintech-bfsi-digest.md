## Signals

**India macro — Hormuz is the key risk to watch.** A sustained Hormuz disruption translates directly into: (1) higher India oil import bill → wider CAD → INR pressure → RBI dilemma; (2) fertiliser import disruption → Kharif season agricultural risk → food inflation. These are not remote tail risks — shipping is already at 90% below normal. This should be tracked as the primary macro risk variable in the near term.

**India corporate earnings holding up.** 42% beat rate and 16% sales growth suggests the feared macro slowdown hasn't fully hit the corporate P&L. This aligns with Bajaj Finance's observation of strong consumer loan demand in April/May. India's domestic demand story appears intact — for now.

**IT sector structural shift is underway.** "The outsourcing arbitrage that built Indian IT is being replaced by a different arbitrage — model inference vs. human hours." Entry-level headcount across TCS, Infosys, Wipro, HCL is under structural pressure. This is a revenue deflation story, not a cyclical one — margins may hold but revenue growth will compress.

**US fintech embedded banking risk crystallised.** Parker's bankruptcy is a cautionary tale: even well-funded fintechs (Valar/YC pedigree) can collapse instantly when their banking partner withdraws. Patriot Bank's move is a signal of broader risk appetite reduction in US embedded banking relationships.

**Crypto regulatory arc diverging.** US is moving toward stablecoin legitimisation (CLARITY Act). Brazil is moving in the opposite direction (crypto FX settlement ban). EM central banks protecting FX systems vs. US trying to maintain dollar dominance through regulated stablecoins.

**Paytm vs. Bajaj Finance (Morning Context):** The question "how will Paytm battle Bajaj Finance without an NBFC licence?" is the key strategic framing for Paytm's lending ambitions. BFL's combination of ₹5 lakh crore AUM, 119M customers, NBFC licence, and FinAI is a compounding moat.
